About The Verified Brief — and the one rule that makes it different

You are drowning in confident AI writing, and almost none of it tells you what it doesn't know.

That's not a small problem. As AI floods every feed with fluent, plausible, unverifiable claims, "sounds right" has detached from "is right" — and in the places where being wrong is expensive (building a company, buying a tool, making a call with real money on it), that gap will cost you. The market calls it a hallucination crisis. We call it the opening.

The Verified Brief is intelligence that survives its own cross-examination.

Here is the one rule, and everything follows from it: every factual claim we publish is put through an adversarial verification pass before it reaches you. A claim doesn't get printed because it sounds true or because it's circulating — it gets printed because it survived a process designed to refute it. Concretely:

  • Every claim is tagged ✅ verified (held up against primary or reputable sources), ⚠️ soft (directional — real but single-source or vendor-run, and labeled as such), or ❌ refuted (we tried to confirm it and it failed — so we tell you it's false instead of repeating it).
  • Every claim carries its source.
  • We publish what we don't know. When a number can't be confirmed at the source, we say so and leave it out rather than dress a guess as a fact. The method note at the bottom of each issue lists what we excluded and why.

That last part is the whole thing. Anyone can sound certain. We'd rather be trusted — which means showing you the seams, flagging the soft spots, and naming what we threw on the floor. We will be wrong sometimes; when we are, we'll mark it and correct it in the open. Trust isn't a tone here. It's the product.

What we cover: the AI and autonomous-agent frontier — what's genuinely real versus what's marketed, where the money actually is, what the law actually says, and which viral numbers don't survive a second look.

Who it's for: people building, buying, or deciding in AI who can't afford to be wrong.

One verified brief a week. Quality over frequency — a single bad fact would undo the only thing we're selling.
